Ten things we know to be true

One of the earliest culture setting activities we did at CFS was writing a list of "ten things we know to be true". This activity was likely suggested by one of our advisors who was an early engineering leader at Google.

I found that writing out these ten things to be a more powerful and useful activity than a standard set of company values, as is often done. The problem with listing company values is they are all too often generic and non-differentiating (e.g., very few companies would not have integrety or respect). Writing the things you know to be true goes much deeper. It is an exercise in world building and culture creation. It is the logical next step after writing out your audacious goals, which describe what your company is going to accomplish. The things you know to be true lays out the frameworks and methedology by which your company works to achieve its audacious goals.

CFS' ten things has not yet been published publicly, so I cannot examine it in detail here (I will if it ever is made public). But, the major themes shouldn't surprise you: recognizing the power of the scientific method, applying innovation where it has the highest leverage, predicting that human creativity and inexepnsive energy will drive the future, etc. Instead, I will examine Google's ten things as an exercise to help you build yours (see the link for Google's explinaions of each truth).

1. Focus on the user and all else will follow.

This is a great anchor truth to start Google's list. It provides a clear focus when making decisions: does it focus on the user? If so, then it reinforces more user engagement. For your anchor truth, what is the most important hueristic that you can come up with to clearly drive your decisions?

2. It’s best to do one thing really, really well.

Google's second truth sets the tone on the breadth of what they work on. While this was a great guiding truth at the begining, in my opinion they have not stuck to this one. The company has such an impressive breadth in what they offer, it is a streatch to say that they focus on doing only one thing really well. Nonetheless, this evolution shows that it is okay for your truths to evolve over time, as there is a risk of being disruptive if you stay static. Periodically revisit your truths and check to see if they are still serving you as the world changes.

3. Fast is better than slow.

The third truth is a derivative of the first: delivering what the user wants. A slow product is frustrating to use and would drive away their user base. This truth is counter to the philosophy of the search industry in the early 2000's, which was to try to capture the user's attention as long as possible. This is a great example of Peter Theil's signature question "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"

4. Democracy on the web works.

The fourth truth gets at the heart of what makes Google's technology, the PageRank™ algorithm, work. It was their unique insight that the "voting" of the links between websites was a superior way of identifying quality search results. What is the simple truth behind your hard-won innovation?

5. You don’t need to be at your desk to need an answer.

The fifth truth was very forward looking. These truths were first penned years before the iPhone, when most of us who had cellphones had very simple phones. It is impressive that they already had the foresight of the importance of mobile computing and access to information. For your truths: What does a future look like with your technology? What does your technology enable? How does your technology change with other innovations?

6. You can make money without doing evil.

The sixth truth is, like the third, a reaction against outside industry trends. The exposition focuses on how Google deploys ads in its products, its primary source of revenue. As the advertisement industry, through its goal of getting a consumer to buy a prodcut that it potentially does not need, is often portratyed in a negative context. Google set out this truth to lay out how it earns money through ads while doing it ethically. Having an ethical framework is an important part of just about any professional field (e.g., the Hippocratic Oath in medicine or the Obligation of an Engineer). For your field, what is an important ethical truth you should hold yourself to?

This truth also provides an important cautionary tale: Google should be lauded for making their truths public. Public truths drive accountability. If you claim to hold yourself to an ethical standard and appear to fall short, people will speak up about it.

7. There’s always more information out there.

The seventh truth is another forward-looking innovation truth. It recognizes that while Google's first major innovation and product was search on internet webpages, its business and technology extended far beyond. Google was not just an internet search engine, its core skill and product for consumers was the aggrigation of information and returning the best results for the user based on their query. Looking beyond what you are doing today, what is the core truth at the center of what you are doing and delivering?

8. The need for information crosses all borders.

The eigth truth sets the expanse of who the company serves. Google recognized the relative boarderlessness of the internet, the utility of its tools, and the need for information accross previously hard to cross barriers. For your truths, what is the ultimate potential expanse of its impact?

9. You can be serious without a suit.

The ninth truth is a core expression of Google's way of working. While classic Silicon Valley startup in many ways, it was nonetheless important to make explicit. This is a pragmatic expression of their vision of a work environment that was most conducive to getting a productive workforce. For your truths, what workplace culture do you envision is most conducive to your team working together and producing your product effectively?

10. Great just isn’t good enough.

The final truth is a rallying call for continual improvement. In it Google communicates to its team members to always be on the lookout for opportunities to improve, setting an important tone for the culture of how they work. It also communicates to its users that it will always be working to be better than the competition, keeping them engaged rather than going astray. What truths help keep both your team as well as your customers engaged in the right behavior?
